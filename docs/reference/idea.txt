## Monolith vs microservices — the honest take

For where you are right now, **start with a monolith**. Here's why:

Microservices solve problems of scale — when 10+ engineers are stepping on each other's code, when one service is getting 100x more traffic than others, or when you need to deploy parts independently. You have none of these problems yet. Companies like Shopify, Stack Overflow, and Basecamp ran monoliths for years at massive scale. Premature microservices will slow you down by 3–4x in the early stages — you'll spend more time on infrastructure than on features.

**The right approach:** Build a well-structured monolith with clean internal module boundaries, so that when you do need to split, each module peels off cleanly into its own service. This is called a "modular monolith" and it's exactly what most successful startups do.

### Key design decisions in this schema worth understanding

The `itineraries` table has both a `jsonb items` column and a separate `itinerary_items` table. Use the `jsonb` for fast reads when displaying a list of itineraries (no joins needed), and use the normalized `itinerary_items` table when you need to query or filter by category, price range, or booking URL. This is a common pattern called "denormalization for reads."

The `user_plan_items` table is a copy of `itinerary_item` data, not just a foreign key reference. This is deliberate — when a user edits their plan (changes a hotel, removes a day), you don't want to touch the original itinerary. They're editing their own copy.

The `likes` table has a unique constraint on `(user_id, itinerary_id)` — enforced at the database level, not just in application code. This prevents double-likes even if your API gets called twice simultaneously.

The `orders` table stores `razorpay_order_id` as the link back to the payment provider. Never store card data yourself — Razorpay handles that. Your `status` field cycles through `pending → paid → refunded`.

---

### How to migrate from monolith to microservices (the safe path)

The migration order matters a lot. You should split in this exact sequence, because each step is independently deployable without breaking anything:

1. Extract the notification service first — it has no incoming dependencies, only outgoing. Safest split.
2. Extract the AI service second — it's stateless and self-contained.
3. Extract the user service — everything depends on it, but it changes rarely once stable.
4. Extract search — point Typesense at a read replica of your Postgres data.
5. Split itinerary, social, plan, and booking last — these are the most interconnected.

The message bus (Redis pub/sub to start, Kafka when you need durability) is what lets services talk without direct HTTP calls. For example: when a user likes an itinerary, the Social service publishes a `like.created` event. The Notification service listens for that event and sends a push notification. Neither service knows about the other — they just talk through events.

**Itinsy** is the most conceptually similar globally — it's a community of travelers who share itineraries and inspire others, with filtering by destination, month, length of stay and trip type. **Tripoto** is the Indian competitor to watch — it's a Delhi-based startup that allows users to create itineraries for upcoming trips and share them with other travelers, and connects travelers from around the world to share and discover travel destinations. It has scale in India but focuses on travel stories/blogs rather than structured priced itineraries. No copy-to-plan, no itemized costs, no booking.

**TravelTriangle** connects travelers to multiple local travel agents who provide quotations — packages are customizable at the time of booking. It's agent-driven, not community-driven. No social layer, no ranking.
